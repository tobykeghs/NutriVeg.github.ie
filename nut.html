The term ‘nut allergy’ can seem confusing, because we tend to use it to describe an allergy to the fruit of unrelated plants such as peanuts, seeds and nuts that grow on trees. Peanuts are related to chickpeas and peas, whereas tree nuts include almonds, cashews, macadamia nuts and walnuts. Seeds include sesame seeds, sunflower seeds and coconuts. Peanut allergy is the most common allergies in children and although they can outgrow it, for some it will become worse. People who are allergic to peanuts doesn't necessarily mean one will be allergic to tree nuts or seeds.

Symptoms of nut allergies

  • raised red bumps of skin – hives (urticaria)
  • swelling of the lips
  • tingling of the throat and mouth
  • itchy skin and rash
  • runny nose

Causes of nut allergies

For all allergies, the immune system reacts to specific allergy trigger molecules (allergens). Your immune system, or that of a child in your care, produces antibodies that detect the allergen and cause inflammatory reactions and the release of a chemical called histamine. Histamine causes hives, hay fever and other allergic symptoms. The molecules that trigger your allergic reaction can be present in a range of foods, and you may have an allergic reaction to foods containing that molecule. For this reason, some people are allergic to the same allergy trigger in cashews and pistachios. This is known as cross-reactivity. Speak to your doctor about cross-reactivity because it is difficult to predict.

Treatment for nut allergies

The only treatment for food allergies is to avoid the food that causes your allergy. Even if you are careful, it is difficult to avoid all contact with a specific food.
